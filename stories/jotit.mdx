<h2>About</h2>

<div style={{ fontSize: '1rem', lineHeight: 1.8, color: 'var(--ss-text-secondary)' }}>

Most school software makes students bend around it. Jotit's Android app had been built the other way — shaped around how students actually work, writing by hand, annotating, sorting material as they go. The team had put years into that product, and it showed in the traction they'd built.

The problem was that school districts were going iPad-first, fast, and Jotit didn't have an iPad app. Every district that made the switch was a deal Jotit couldn't chase. They needed iOS, and they needed it soon.

That's when they called Applift. They already knew what they needed: someone to help make the architectural calls right, early, and under a tight deadline. The kind of decisions you don't want to revisit two years later.

</div>

<h2>The Build</h2>

<div style={{ fontSize: '1rem', lineHeight: 1.8, color: 'var(--ss-text-secondary)' }}>

Jotit knew their product and their users cold. What they didn't have was an iOS team. Applift did, and more importantly, they'd done this kind of move before. The tricky part of porting a mature product isn't the screens; it's knowing which things need to be rethought on the new platform and which don't.

The first weeks weren't about writing code yet. They were about making a handful of calls that would be expensive to undo: which architectural patterns to commit to, where to diverge from the Android app, which shortcuts were worth taking and which weren't. Applift had enough iOS context to push back where it mattered.

Once building started, both teams fell into a steady cadence. Product scope, sprint priorities, what to ship now versus later — those were joint conversations. Applift helped shape the product, not just ship it.

The app came together in two stages. First, the native iOS scaffolding: the under-the-hood parts that separate a real iPad app from a port. Then the product layer on top — the gestures, the editing flows, the interactions that make sense on iPad specifically, rather than as a direct translation from Android. What shipped wasn't a mirror of the Android app. It was Jotit, designed for iPad from the ground up.

</div>

<h2>The Outcome</h2>

<div style={{ fontSize: '1rem', lineHeight: 1.8, color: 'var(--ss-text-secondary)' }}>

For Jotit, this wasn't a side bet. iPad had already become the default in a lot of their market, and without an iOS app they were quietly losing accounts they'd otherwise have won. The launch closed that gap, and it closed it without the usual tradeoffs of a rushed port.

Past the app itself, Jotit came out of the project with a second platform in the field and an iOS codebase worth investing in further. The thing that started as an urgent deadline is now part of what they're building on.

</div>
