## The Outcome

Over four years with RiseUp, we **shipped three apps across two markets** and **built the internal mobile team** that owns the codebase today. The UK build was native iOS and Android, dense and animation-heavy, written in SwiftUI and Jetpack Compose. The Israeli launch was the one users had been waiting for. A community that had been on the web product for years, and had been asking for a dedicated app for almost as long, finally got one. We built it in Flutter, with in-app purchase flows tailored to the market.

We did three things at once that most agencies split into separate engagements. We architected and shipped the apps. We recruited and onboarded RiseUp's internal mobile engineers. And we stayed embedded long enough that by the time we stepped back, the team could keep building without us.

## Bridging the Mobile Gap

RiseUp came to us at the start of 2022. They had already built a trusted financial wellness platform, but their users were on mobile, and the web product couldn't keep up. They weren't looking for a vendor to execute a spec. They needed an **engineering partner** who could set the **architectural foundation** for a mobile product that would grow into new features and new markets.

After working through the roadmap with their team, we committed to a **fully native approach**. Flutter or React Native would have shipped faster, but RiseUp's product was dense, highly interactive, and heavy on motion. Platform-specific fidelity mattered. On iOS that meant SwiftUI. On Android, Jetpack Compose. Both modern, both declarative, both chosen because we knew we'd be leaning on animation and polish, and we didn't want to fight our tools to get it.

## Building the Platform and the Team

We embedded a squad of **senior mobile engineers** directly inside RiseUp, operating as an extension of their product team rather than an outside shop delivering tickets. That distinction matters. It's the reason the next part worked.

Through 2023, while the native builds were in progress, we ran **technical recruiting and onboarding** for RiseUp's internal mobile group in parallel. New hires came into a codebase that already had standards, conventions, and real code review happening against it. Architectural decisions got shared in real time instead of inherited from a doc. **The team and the platform matured together**, which is the only way that handoff actually works later.

In 2024 we shipped the native iOS and Android apps for the UK market. This was the build we'd been setting ourselves up to make from day one: dense flows, heavy animation work, screen transitions that don't survive a cross-platform wrapper. SwiftUI and Jetpack Compose gave our engineers room to do the detail work. The **micro-interactions, the easing curves, the state transitions**. It's native iOS and Android development at its most demanding, and it's the build we point to when someone asks what our mobile team can actually do.

With the UK live and the internal team established, we turned to Israel. These users had been on the web product for years and had been asking for an app for most of it. Working alongside RiseUp's now-established engineers, we built a Flutter application for the Israeli audience in 2025 and implemented localized in-app purchase flows to support it. The team that had just shipped two native apps could step into a cross-platform build without losing a beat, because they'd already lived on both sides.

## A Mobile Practice Built to Keep Growing

Four years of work comes down to three things. We shipped native iOS and Android apps for the UK market, built in SwiftUI and Jetpack Compose at the level that product called for. We shipped the Israeli Flutter app that users had been waiting on for years. And we built the **internal mobile team** that owns all of it now.

That last one was always the point. We didn't come in to hand over a binary and leave. We came in to build a mobile practice inside RiseUp, and the way we did that, with recruiting, code review, architecture, and shipping happening in the same room and with the same people, is what made the other two possible.
